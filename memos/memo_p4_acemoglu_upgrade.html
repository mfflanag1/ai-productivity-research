Results Memo — Project 4: The Acemoglu Upgrade (Measured Bottom-Up AI TFP Potential)

2026-06-10. Workstream W7. Code: notebooks/21_acemoglu_upgrade.py (consumes the W6 dataset). Outputs: notebooks/output/acemoglu_upgrade/. Pre-registered spec: PAPER_NOTES.md §7 (locked 2026-06-10).

Question

Acemoglu (2024, "The Simple Macroeconomics of AI") computed AI's ten-year TFP potential (~0.66%) from two imputed inputs: Eloundou-style exposure shares (~20% of tasks) × one lab study's time savings (27%). The Anthropic Economic Index now measures both. What does the same calculation give with measured inputs?

Inputs, measured vs imputed

Input Acemoglu (imputed) This memo (measured)
Task time savings 27% (one lab study) 90% mean / 91% median per instance (AEI human_only_time vs human_with_ai_time, 2,707 task-occupation rows)
Eligible task coverage ~20% of tasks (exposure scores) 74.1% of the 2019 wage bill (AEI-observed tasks × O*NET importance × OES wage bills; unmerged tasks count as ZERO savings — conservative)
Usage incidence adoption path assumption BTOS firm adoption 20.6% × 45% worker adoption = 9.3% anchor (both in-repo, cited)

Results

Quantity Value
CEILING TFP (all eligible task work AI-assisted) 39.4%
REALIZED-now TFP (current incidence anchor) 3.6%
Acemoglu (2024), ten-year 0.66%

Break-size mapping (links to W1/W4): spread over a 10-year diffusion, the ceiling implies +3.9%/yr on the TFP increment — ~49x the electrification-era regime shift, beyond any historical transition; the realized-now number implies +0.36%/yr — ~5x the electrification shift, i.e., electrification-class if sustained.

The finding is the bracket, not either endpoint

The ceiling's absurd size is itself the key diagnostic: per-instance measured savings (90%, on tasks users chose to bring to Claude) cannot be applied to all task work as an average treatment effect. Selection into usage — people bring Claude precisely the tasks (and task-portions) where it is fast — is the dominant wedge between AEI-measured savings and Acemoglu's lab-calibrated input.

The defensible headline is therefore:

Replacing imputed exposure with measured eligibility and imputed savings with measured per-instance savings raises the bottom-up TFP estimate from 0.66% to 3.6% at current usage incidence (adopter-ATT, not ATE) — and the gap between the 3.6% realized and 39% ceiling numbers is a measurement of how much selection-into-usage inflates naive extrapolations of observed AI productivity gains.

Caveats (pre-registered)

  • Adopter-ATT, not ATE: savings conditional on self-selected usage.
  • Single AEI week (Nov 13–20, 2025); pool vintages before quoting levels externally.
  • AEI durations are model-estimated (validated by Anthropic against lab studies, median abs. error < 7 minutes).
  • Within-occupation wage bills apportioned by O*NET importance; 13 occupations with missing importance filled at the global median.
  • No input-output amplification (matches Acemoglu's task-based setup); labor share 0.6.

Next steps

  1. Quality adjustment: weight savings by AEI task-success rates (a success-weighted ceiling).
  2. Pool the Aug'25/Feb'26 vintages for savings stability (W6 panel infrastructure already built).
  3. Selection bound: pair AEI per-instance savings with external ATE estimates (e.g., RCT literature) to turn the bracket into a formal partial-identification interval.
  4. BEA input-output aggregation as a sensitivity.

Extensions (2026-06-10, notebooks/23_acemoglu_extensions.py)

Spec locked in PAPER_NOTES.md (W7 extension) before estimation. Outputs in notebooks/output/acemoglu_upgrade/ (table_quality_adjusted, table_vintage_stability, table_selection_bounds, fig_selection_bounds).

  1. Quality adjustment. Mean task success rate 75.6% (86% facet coverage). Success-weighting cuts the ceiling 39.3% → 29.7% (realized-now 3.65% → 2.76%); the conservative variant (failures also waste the AI interaction time) gives 28.7% / 2.66%.
  2. Vintage stability. 1,876 tasks measured in both Nov-2025 and Feb-2026: per-task savings correlate 0.82; mean savings 89.9% vs 90.0%; headline ceiling drift −0.2% on the common subsample. The single-week caveat is largely retired for the savings input. (Schema drift handled: Feb-2026 appends ::value to time-facet cluster names; extractor now vintage-proof.)
  3. Selection bounds (partial identification). Replacing the per-instance 90% with external RCT ATEs: realized-now TFP ∈ [0.58%, 3.65%] — and the lower bound (Brynjolfsson-Li-Raymond 14% field estimate) essentially recovers Acemoglu's 0.66%. His number is the conservative-ATE corner of the measured calculation. With the crude Domar factor (×1.9): [1.1%, 6.9%].
  4. Is it showing up yet? (descriptive). Fernald utilization-adjusted TFP growth averaged 0.86%/yr over 2023–2026Q1 vs 0.47%/yr over 2010–2019 — a +0.39pp/yr gap, at/above the upper end of the implied realized-now path (+0.06 to +0.36pp/yr). Three years, COVID-adjacent, no causal claim — but measured TFP acceleration is in the right ballpark for the measured-inputs estimate, and W4's "no detected break yet" remains true (this is increment drift, not a regime shift).

Updated headline: quality-adjusted, vintage-stable, selection-bounded realized-now TFP of [0.6%, 2.8%], with Acemoglu's 0.66% as the conservative corner and current measured TFP drift consistent with the interval.

Realized-now TFP band: [0.58%, 1.43%] — Acemoglu's 0.66% sits inside it. The verification-tax δ (how much augmentation-mode usage saves) is not identified; the band, not a point, is the honest object.

Two standalone findings: - The non-market wedge: only 46.3% of task-level AI usage is work (34.7% personal, 19.0% coursework). More than half of measured AI task activity produces surplus that will never appear in TFP — first-order for interpreting any TFP-based AI assessment. - Wedges are vintage-stable (Nov→Feb: work 46.3%→45.4%, automation 47.2%→45.9%); the verification tax is not yet shrinking.

Reconciliation: the Fernald 2023–26 TFP drift (+0.39pp/yr) now sits ABOVE the decomposed band's ten-year path (+0.06 to +0.14pp/yr) — i.e., if the acceleration is real and AI-driven, it exceeds what the fully-wedged bottom-up calculation explains; descriptive, COVID-adjacent.

Tracking the wedges (2026-06-10, notebooks/25_verification_reinstatement.py)

  • Verification tax over 14 months (5 vintages): automation share 42.6% → 45.9% net, but non-monotonic and method-inconsistent across the v1/v2→v3 boundary; within the consistent v3 segment it FELL (51.6% Aug'25 → 45.9% Feb'26). The verification tax is not measurably falling — plausibly a composition effect (usage expanding into harder, more collaborative tasks as capability rises); shift-share decomposition queued.
  • Reinstatement, measured honestly: AEI's own taxonomy non-coverage is stable at ~7% of usage (8.1→6.5→7.0). The earlier "13.7% unmatched = new tasks" reading is retracted: fuzzy-matching shows median similarity 0.96 to ONET 30.1 statements — it is mostly ONET version drift.

The scaffolding dividend (2026-06-10, notebooks/26_scaffolding_dividend.py)

Same O*NET tasks, same model families, two deployment contexts (AEI 1P API = scaffolded production use vs Claude.ai consumer use), three vintages:

  • Within-task automation gap (API − consumer): +30.1pp usage-weighted (median +45.5pp; 89% of 1,322 common tasks more automated on API). Aggregate automation share: API 81–87% vs consumer 46–52%. Deployment context alone moves the verification tax by 30–45pp.
  • The flat/declining consumer automation share is NOT a composition artifact. Shift-share (Aug'25→Feb'26): consumer within-task −3.8pp vs composition −1.9pp; API within −5.5pp vs −0.6pp. Within the same task, interactions became MORE collaborative on both platforms while models improved — candidate stories (user-base composition, complexity creep within task categories, product-surface changes) are logged; the aggregates cannot separate them.
  • Waterfall implication: the API automation level is the measured headroom of scaffolding. Shifting work usage to API-style deployment lifts the verification lower bound of realized-now TFP from 0.58% to ≈1.0% with no model improvement — the scaffolding dividend is the near-term TFP frontier, and it is already deployed in one segment of the market.

Completing the verification-tax story (2026-06-10, notebooks/27_decline_decomposition.py)

Why is within-task automation falling? Not user composition. On consumer, within-task user-skill changes do nothing (+0.004, p=.59) while instance-complexity creep is real (tasks whose instances got bigger became less automated, −0.047, p=.035) and growing tasks became more automated. On API the complexity sign flips (+0.056): scaffolding converts bigger jobs into delegation; its decline is mostly an unexplained common drift (new agentic use-cases onboarding collaboratively / product mix).

The verifiability gradient. Consumer levels confirm the theory cleanly: automation 58% (verifiable)

43% (middle) > 32% (unverifiable). But the dividend is LARGEST for unverifiable tasks (+44pp vs +26pp) and the API gradient is compressed — deployment context substitutes for machine-verifiability. The refined verification-tax claim: the tax binds at the interactive margin and is bought out wholesale by deployment infrastructure, not task-by-task by checkability.

Baumol-within, quantified: machine-verifiable tasks are only 14.7% of the wage bill (unverifiable 11.5%, middle 73.8%). The TFP action is in the middle mass — which is exactly where the scaffolding dividend operates.
