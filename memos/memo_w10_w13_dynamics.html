The question

W9 produced a projected realized-TFP path with two free legs: the capability scenarios (parameterized, not forecast) and three stated-but-unmodeled limitations (selection unwind, GE composition, the capital-side accounting). The four extensions close those gaps with measured or explicitly-bounded machinery and stand up the apparatus that adjudicates the scenarios in public. The joint question: which W9 conclusions survive when its own caveats are modeled?

1. Selection unwind (W10a): the long run halves; the near-term test survives

Mixture/ATT model: inframarginal usage keeps the measured 90% savings; marginal adopters earn the RCT-anchored ATE (central 27%, band 15–56%). Nests W9 exactly at s_RCT = 0.90; anchor and κ calibration untouched.

The asymmetry is the finding: the 2027 coverage-channel prediction is robust to selection unwind (every central cell still crosses +0.5 pp/yr in 2027, because early acceleration is inframarginal — g_sel ≈ 1 while A ≈ A₀), while the long-run levels are not (the 2030 unlock peak damps 2.19 → 1.21 pp/yr). Selection is a levels problem, not a timing problem. W9's headline should now be quoted as a two-layer band: [unwind-27 lower envelope, W9 upper envelope].

Two readings, both recorded: (a) the capability unlock is arriving early, in which case the W9 "unlock" scenario is conservative on timing; (b) selection into easier tasks within the quarter (composition, not capability) — distinguishable in the next vintage by the success-by-complexity profile. 3. GE on measured tasks (W11): the index is a good map, with three quantified corrections

The 2,707 measured tasks in a static CES economy, automation driven by the W9 wedge paths (not cost decline — W6), closed form, 64 paths. σ=1 reconciles with the W9 index within ~3% where S < 5%; all anchors (W7b ceilings 1e-9, W7e class shares exact) hold.

  1. The Baumol drag (σ = 0.5, unlock, lower bound): −0.26pp (2030), −1.77pp (2035), −2.77pp (2040) of aggregate gain vs the fixed-share benchmark; −4.0pp at σ = 0.2 by 2040. Task complementarity is a first-order long-run damper — same shape as selection: spares the near-term, takes from the far end.
  2. Jensen's correction, the other way: the exact CD aggregate rises above the linear index as task-level cuts deepen (−ln λ convex); by 2035 σ=1 GE exceeds the index by 15% (lo) / 63% (hi). The multiplicative index understates the exact aggregate at deep utilization — the two corrections partially offset.
  3. Composition and wages: the pre-registered Baumol-within-cognitive-work composition shift is directionally confirmed and quantitatively second-order (+0.1pp unverifiable expenditure share by 2035 — class-mean λ differences are diluted by work-share and utilization). No-mobility wage bounds spread as predicted but mildly: verifiable 0.91, unverifiable 1.01 by 2035.
  4. Labor share: flat, as pre-registered. At measured parity (~636×), AI's income claim is ≤ 0.04% of sector cost in 2035. Displacement appears as task reallocation, not labor-share decline — a sharp, falsifiable departure from capital-deepening intuitions.

4. The measured J-curve (W12): the trough is real in exactly the corners that matter

Increment-form residual: residual_AI(t) = ΔS(t) − α_AI·g_KAI(t) − β_AI·Δln(1+(μ−1)A(t)), 120 combos (5 W9 corners × selection × 3 capex paths × 4 α_AI).

  • The permanent trough is live: the conservative corner never flips in any of its 24 combos; 42/120 combos never cross by 2040. "AI capex permanently outruns measured AI productivity" is a real scenario, not a rhetorical one.
  • The genuine J-curves are the verification-lower-bound central corners: crossover 2030 (sustained capex, α = 0.05), 2029 (adoption-tied), and unwind-27 pushes it past 2040. The hi-band corners flip immediately (no trough at all).
  • Sensitivity ordering of the crossover year: W9 corner = α_AI (14-yr spread each) ≫ selection (11) ≫ capex path (3). What you assume about capability/verification and about the capital elasticity matters; the capex path barely does.
  • The headline demolition: the income-share-consistent capital elasticity is α_AI(2026) = 0.0065 — 7.7× below the conventional 0.05. Measured capex/GDP (1.3%, δ = 0.25, r = 0.05) supports a 0.23pp capital drag today, not the 1.75pp our own AA-TFP dashboard (and the public conversation) carries. Under decelerating capex it never reaches 0.02. Implication: the measured J-curve trough is shallow — the "AI is dragging measured TFP down 1.75pp" story overstates the input side by nearly an order of magnitude, which cuts both ways: less drag now, but also less mechanical rebound later.

Joint reading

Stack the four extensions and the W9 scenario space compresses asymmetrically:

  • Robust: the 2027 coverage-channel test (survives selection, GE composition, and the capital-side reaccounting — every damper is a long-run damper); the verification band as the honest object; the adjudication dates (2027Q4, then each vintage).
  • Fragile: all long-run levels. S(2035) under {unwind × Baumol-σ=0.5} spans roughly 2–11% where W9 alone said 5–17%. Anyone quoting the upper paths without the unwind and σ caveats is now contradicting the program's own extensions.
  • Updated priors: the quality wedge moved a quarter after we parameterized it as flat — the one direction W9's unlock scenario did not anticipate is early capability arrival; and the capital-drag story everyone (including our dashboard) tells is ~7× too big on measured income shares.

Limitations

W10's vintage test has one time-step (power arrives with each release); the unwind multiplier is aggregate (RCT anchors are not task-level); W11's σ is locked-but-assumed (0.5 primary) and its mobility cases are polar bounds; W12's findings are conditional on W9 corners by construction; W13 is descriptive by design. The adjudication rule is mechanical — corner flips trigger human [RESULT] entries, not automatic reinterpretation.
