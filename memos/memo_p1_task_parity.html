Results Memo — Project 1: Task-Level Cost Parity and AI Usage

2026-06-10. Workstream W6 (cross-section + AEI-vintage panel). Code: notebooks/20_task_parity.py, notebooks/22_task_parity_panel.py, src/task_parity.py. Outputs: notebooks/output/task_parity/. Pre-registered specs: PAPER_NOTES.md §6 (locked 2026-06-10 before estimation).

Question

Does the human-vs-AI cost-parity gap predict task-level AI usage intensity — the cost-barrier → diffusion-speed law (elasticity ≈ −1 across 30 technologies, 1605–1983) rebuilt at the task level with measured inputs?

Data

  • Usage and time measures: Anthropic Economic Index, O*NET task facets. Cross-section: Nov 2025 week (3,170 tasks; usage shares, human_only_time [hours], human_with_ai_time [minutes], task success). Units verified against the published "AI reduces task time by 80% on average."
  • Task → occupation: O*NET Task Statements (86.3% exact-text merge; 535 unmatched tasks reported), importance from Task Ratings.
  • Wages/employment: OES 2019 vintage (in-repo; BLS blocks scripted current downloads — relative occupation wages are highly persistent; swap queued).
  • Panel: five public AEI vintages — Dec'24/Jan'25 (v1), Feb/Mar'25 (v2), Aug'25, Nov'25, Feb'26 — harmonized at the normalized task-text layer. Balanced panel: 1,869 tasks × 5 vintages.

Estimation sample (cross-section): 2,707 task-occupation rows, 2,063 tasks, 457 occupations. Median human/AI cost-parity ratio ≈ 636x (p10 232x, p90 1,439x). Median measured time savings 91%.

Results

Cross-section (within-occupation, SEs clustered by occupation)

Spec β (log parity gap) 95% CI n
No controls +0.216 [+0.093, +0.338] 2,615
Primary: + task-success control −0.030 [−0.164, +0.103] 2,215
Diagnostic: unnormalized usage +0.187 [+0.064, +0.310] 2,615

The raw gradient has the predicted sign; the capability control absorbs it entirely. Cost advantage and capability are collinear across tasks — the cross-section cannot separate them.

Coarse placebo: the wage gradient of AI activity is ~6x steeper now (AEI usage elasticity 1.49) than the pre-2022 exposure-score gradient (0.24). (The intended yearly placebo failed: pct_ai in the 2011–2019 panel is a static exposure score, not a yearly series.)

Panel (first differences, vintage effects, task-clustered)

Identification: tasks assigned time-invariant price tiers (terciles of task size); commodity-tier prices collapsed $1.25 → $0.10/1M tokens (−92%) across the window while frontier stayed flat at $15. In first differences, time-invariant task capability drops out.

FD price elasticity of usage: −0.009 (se 0.010), 95% CI [−0.030, +0.012]; cluster p = 0.39; tier-permutation p = 0.43. Leave-one-vintage-out: −0.007 to −0.038 (the larger values when dropping early vintages, where a pricing-table composition artifact lives — see Threats).

Interpretation — the thesis this supports

Putting the two legs together with W5 (AI's input-cost barrier = 0.022, below the entire historical support; only the bits-distribution floor remains):

At parity gaps of ~600x, cost has ceased to bind. Task-level AI usage composition is capability-constrained, not price-constrained. The historical cost-barrier law (−1 elasticity) was estimated on technologies near their cost margins at launch; AI is the first technology in the sample's range to launch past its cost margin, and the law's task-level analogue correctly goes quiet there.

This reframes the JMP from "the cost-barrier law at the task level" to "the cost-barrier law, and where it stops applying" — with the cross-section, the panel, and the W5 barrier accounting as the three mutually consistent pieces of evidence.

Threats and limitations (all logged)

  1. Selection into Claude usage — usage measures Claude-user task composition, not economy-wide adoption. Occupation FE + footprint normalization mitigate within the data; Census BTOS provides the firm-margin external anchor.
  2. Tier price paths are built from a sparse 15-row pricing table — the mid tier shows a composition artifact (representative model switches between v1 and v2). Fix queued: per-model price histories (Artificial Analysis; AEI 1P API model mix).
  3. Tier-correlated capability drift (frontier models improving faster on long tasks) survives first-differencing; Δtask-success controls possible only on the v3-vintage subset.
  4. Single-week vintages; AEI time facets are model-estimated; OES 2019 wages; taxonomy drift handled at the task-text layer (match rates reported per vintage pair).

Next steps

  1. Per-model price histories → rebuilt task-level cost paths (kills the composition artifact).
  2. Event-study around specific dated price cuts rather than vintage-to-vintage differences.
  3. Swap May-2024+ OES wages when fetchable; pool second snapshot weeks when published.
  4. BTOS industry-level external validation of the capability-constrained interpretation.
